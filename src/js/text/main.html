Where political parties get their money: unmasking associated entities

Some of the largest donors to the two main political parties in Australia weren’t wealthy individuals or businesses – they were companies set up by the parties themselves known as “associated entities”. So what are they, and where do they get their money from?

Here you can see the twenty largest financiers for Australian political parties during the last election, as reported by the parties themselves. The circles are sized by the total amount of money given.

On the right, you can see the largest amount comes from public funding, through the NSW electoral commission. There are also individual donors here, such as mining magnate Paul Marks. On the left are the associated entities. The largest associated entity donor to the Liberal party was the Cormack Foundation, and the largest associated entity donor to the Labor party is was Labor Holdings Pty Ltd.

Technically, an associated entity can be any organisation, so long as it is either controlled by a political party, is a financial member or has voting rights in a political party, or operates wholly (or to a significant extent) to benefit a political party.

In practice, this includes everything from the Free Enterprise Foundation, made infamous for “washing” banned NSW donations from property developers, unions such as the Shop, Distributive and Allied Employees Association, and think-tanks like the Menzies Research Centre.

Here, I’ve mapped out the entities and donors associated with every significant political party, where they get their money from and how much they give to the parties. This allows you to see the flow of money from start to finish, which is ordinarily difficult as each of the parties, entities and donors declare their donations to the Australian Electoral Commission separately.

The total flow of money between each organisation or person are shown by arrows. These transactions are classified by the party or associated entity as a "donation" or "other receipt". The circle outline is coloured depending on that entity's most frequent transaction - "donation", "other receipt" or mixed. The Cormack Foundation was set up decades ago from the proceeds of the sale of Melbourne radio station 3XY and is frequently one of the largest donors to the Liberal party in any financial year. Rather than receiving donations, the Foundation relies on investments to fund the Coalition.

Unusually, in the 2015-16 financial year, it also gave money to Senator David Leyonhjelm’s party, the Liberal Democrats, and Family First.

Labor Holdings is the Labor party’s investment entity and, similar to the Cormack Foundation, was set up from the sale of Labor party radio station 4KQ in 1987. Again, it relies on investments rather than donations to send funds to the Labor parties.

So, how do the two major parties compare?

Here's the federal branch of the Liberal party. Aside from the Cormack Foundation, there is another associated entity sending funds to the party called Parakeelia, which itself receives funds from other branches of the party. It was revealed into 2016 that Liberal MPs and senators pay $2,550 from their office allowance to the Liberal-owned company for software services.

Here's the federal Labor party. Because unions are required to report as associated entities, the network of how funds get to Labor is significantly more complex. Aside from the various unions and Labor Holdings, there is the Chifley Research Centre, which received funds from the NSW branch and transferred funds to the federal branch, and John Curtin House, which reported funds transfers to itself.
